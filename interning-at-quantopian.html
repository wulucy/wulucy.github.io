Interning at Quantopian

Posted on August 21, 2018 in posts

This summer, I interned at Quantopian. (If you're not familiar, Quantopian is a startup that crowdsources trading algorithms; it's backed by Andreessen Horowitz and Bessemer, among others.) Overall, I loved it -- I learned a lot and loved being in Boston.

One of my projects at Quantopian was to create content for the forums. We called this content "case studies": example algorithms, signals, etc. that inspire users.

This summer, I created two content pieces. Both are centered around the same idea: finding signals that are predictive of stock returns in non-traditional data. (By "non-traditional", I pretty much mean not pricing data.) I've put brief descriptions and relevant links below!

1. 10-Ks and 10-Qs

The hypothesis here is from Cohen, Malloy, and Nguyen (2017). In this case study, we're examining whether textual changes (measured via cosine and Jaccard similarity scores) in companies' 10-K and 10-Q filings lead to lower stock returns. The underlying idea here is that companies tend change their 10-Ks and 10-Qs if and only if bad things happen.

In the first post, I scrape 10-Ks and 10-Qs from the SEC EDGAR database, then compute cosine and Jaccard similarity scores based on their text. In the second post, I analyze whether these scores are predictive of higher stock prices.

(After writing most of this case study, I actually got to present my work to one of the paper's authors! Scary, but a really cool experience.)

2. Campaign Contributions

The hypothesis here is from Cooper, Gulen, and Ovtchinnikov (2010). Essentially, we might think that companies who contribute (i.e., donate) more to political candidates gain political favor, and thus show higher stock returns.

In the first post, I pull data from the Federal Election Commission and clean it. As with the 10-K/10-Q case study, the second post contains some analysis of whether the factor is really predictive.

I hope to eventually be able to share some of the other things I worked on this summer, but now is not the time -- I'll link to the other summer-related posts here if they ever happen!
